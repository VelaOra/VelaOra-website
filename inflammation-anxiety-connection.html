Inflammation and Anxiety:
The Connection Nobody's Talking About

✦
Guest Contributor
Integrative Medicine Physician, New Zealand · June 2026 · 9 min read
✦

Guest contributor. This article was written by a New Zealand integrative medicine physician with a clinical focus on women's hormonal health and the role of inflammation in mental wellbeing. She has chosen to remain anonymous so she can speak honestly about what she sees in clinical practice — and what she wishes her patients knew sooner. We're honoured to have her expertise on VelaOra.

In fifteen years of clinical practice, I've noticed a pattern that medical training never adequately prepared me for. Women in their late thirties and forties with new-onset anxiety — no psychological history, no obvious life trigger, partial response to standard treatment — who improve significantly when we address their inflammatory picture rather than their mental health in isolation.

Not every patient. But enough that it's changed how I work. And enough that I think every woman whose anxiety doesn't quite fit the textbook description deserves to hear what I've learned.

The pattern I kept seeing: women who had been through therapy, tried medication, practised mindfulness — all of it helpful to a point, none of it quite getting them to baseline. Women whose anxiety seemed to correlate less with what was happening in their lives and more with how well they'd slept, what they'd eaten, whether they were fighting off something. Women whose bloodwork, when I looked at it through an inflammatory lens rather than a psychiatric one, told a different story.

The connection between chronic inflammation and anxiety is now well-established in the research literature. It is not yet well-established in the GP consultation room. That gap is what this article is about.

What Inflammation Actually Is (And What It Isn't)

Inflammation gets a bad reputation — and most of it is deserved, but not always for the right reasons. Acute inflammation is, in fact, one of your body's most elegant survival mechanisms. You cut your finger. Within minutes, immune cells flood the area, blood vessels dilate, inflammatory proteins are released. The redness, swelling, and heat are your body doing exactly what it's supposed to do. A few days later, everything settles.

The problem is chronic, low-grade inflammation — sometimes called systemic inflammation or "silent" inflammation. This is where the immune system stays in a state of low-level activation long after any acute trigger has passed. It doesn't announce itself with visible swelling. It hums along in the background, releasing inflammatory signalling molecules called cytokines — and those cytokines do not stay in the body. They cross the blood-brain barrier. They talk directly to your brain.

And what they say, it turns out, sounds a lot like anxiety.

The Cytokine-Brain Connection

Here's the science I wish someone had explained to me a decade ago. When inflammatory cytokines — particularly IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α — enter the brain, they trigger a process called neuroinflammation. This isn't damage in the dramatic sense. It's a shift in the way your brain cells communicate.

The Science — How Inflammation Talks to Your Brain

Inflammatory cytokines affect the brain through multiple pathways simultaneously. They activate microglia — the brain's resident immune cells — which then produce their own pro-inflammatory signals. They interfere with the metabolism of tryptophan, the precursor to serotonin. They disrupt the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, amplifying the body's stress response. And they alter glutamate signalling in ways that increase excitatory activity — producing exactly the kind of heightened arousal and hypervigilance we experience as anxiety.

Berk M et al. (2013) BMC Medicine; Bhatt S et al. (2020) Prog Neuropsychopharmacol Biol Psychiatry

What this means practically is that when your body is in a state of chronic inflammation — from diet, gut dysbiosis, environmental toxins, sleep deprivation, or unresolved physiological stress — the downstream effect includes altered neurotransmitter function, a sensitised stress response, and a brain that is primed for threat detection even when there's nothing to detect.

A 2019 meta-analysis of 20 studies found significantly elevated levels of inflammatory markers including IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α in people with anxiety disorders compared to healthy controls. A 2023 review in Frontiers in Psychiatry concluded that the evidence for a bidirectional relationship between neuroinflammation and anxiety is now "compelling and clinically significant." This isn't fringe science. It's mainstream research that is simply slow to filter into the GP consultation room.

Why Women Are Particularly Affected

Women are diagnosed with anxiety disorders at roughly twice the rate of men. The gap appears to widen in perimenopause. And the inflammation story is a significant part of why.

Oestrogen has powerful anti-inflammatory properties. It modulates cytokine production, supports the integrity of the gut lining, and directly influences microglial activity in the brain. When oestrogen is stable — as it broadly is through most of the reproductive years — it acts as a kind of natural buffer between systemic inflammation and the brain.

As oestrogen begins to fluctuate and decline from the mid-thirties onward, that buffer weakens. Inflammatory markers tend to rise. The brain becomes more vulnerable to the anxiogenic effects of cytokine activity. Women who had no significant anxiety history can find themselves, seemingly out of nowhere, with a persistent baseline unease they can't explain or shift.

Oestrogen's Anti-Inflammatory Role

Oestrogen suppresses NF-κB, a key regulator of inflammatory gene expression. It also upregulates anti-inflammatory pathways and maintains gut barrier integrity — one of the primary sites through which systemic inflammation is generated. Its decline in perimenopause directly increases inflammatory burden.

Progesterone and GABA

Progesterone metabolises to allopregnanolone, which enhances GABA receptor sensitivity — GABA being your brain's primary calming neurotransmitter. When progesterone levels drop in the luteal phase, or decline overall in perimenopause, GABA activity decreases and anxiety increases. Inflammation amplifies this by also reducing GABA function independently.

This also helps explain why anxiety so often worsens in the premenstrual phase — when both progesterone and oestrogen drop sharply — and why women who were previously well-managed on antidepressants or anxiolytics sometimes find their medications become less effective in their forties. The underlying inflammatory picture has changed.

The Gut Is at the Centre of This

I know "gut health" has become a wellness cliché, so let me be specific about what I mean here — because it's actually fascinating and directly relevant.

Your gut lining is one of the primary sites where inflammation originates or is contained. A healthy gut lining is selectively permeable — it lets nutrients through and keeps larger particles, bacterial toxins, and undigested food components out. When the gut lining becomes compromised — a condition often called increased intestinal permeability, or colloquially "leaky gut" — bacterial endotoxins including lipopolysaccharides (LPS) cross into the bloodstream. LPS is one of the most potent known triggers of systemic inflammation. It drives up IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP. It activates the same inflammatory cascades that eventually cross the blood-brain barrier and contribute to anxiety and mood dysregulation.

Your gut microbiome also produces approximately 90% of your body's serotonin — and the composition of that microbiome directly influences how much serotonin is produced, how the HPA axis is regulated, and how resilient your brain is to inflammatory signals. A disrupted microbiome isn't just a digestive problem. It has a direct line to your mental state.

The gut is not a separate system from the brain. It is one continuous, bidirectional conversation — and chronic inflammation is one of the loudest things it can say.

— Guest Contributor, VelaOra

Signs Your Anxiety Might Have an Inflammatory Component

I want to be careful here — this is not a diagnostic tool, and anxiety is complex. But these patterns are worth noticing:

Anxiety that has no clear psychological trigger

You're not catastrophising. You don't have a specific worry dominating your thoughts. There's just a background hum of unease or dread that you can't attach to anything. This "free-floating" anxiety is a recognised feature of neuroinflammatory states.

It's worse after poor sleep or dietary slip-ups

Both sleep deprivation and high-sugar or ultra-processed food significantly spike inflammatory markers within 24–48 hours. If you notice your anxiety correlates with these — rather than with external stressors — inflammation may be a contributing driver.

You also have other signs of systemic inflammation

Joint aches, skin issues (acne, eczema, rosacea), frequent illness, bloating, brain fog, fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, and slow recovery from exercise are all potential signs of elevated systemic inflammation. They often co-exist with anxiety for exactly this reason.

Standard anxiety interventions don't fully work

If therapy, mindfulness, and/or medication help partially but never quite get you to baseline — and your anxiety returns reliably even when life is objectively going well — it's worth investigating whether there's an underlying physiological driver that isn't being addressed.

What Actually Helps

The good news is that reducing inflammation and supporting anxiety from this angle involves things that are deeply doable, without a prescription and without overhauling your life overnight. These approaches are also well-researched — I've listed the key references below.

🥗 Food as Information

An anti-inflammatory diet isn't a specific protocol — it's a pattern. The consistent findings across the research point to: abundant vegetables and fruit (especially deeply coloured ones), oily fish two to three times a week, olive oil as your primary fat, legumes, nuts, seeds, and minimal ultra-processed food, refined sugar, and industrial seed oils.

Polyphenols are particularly important. These plant compounds actively suppress NF-κB and reduce pro-inflammatory cytokine production. Berries, dark leafy greens, green tea, turmeric, and — worth noting for anyone in New Zealand — blackcurrants are exceptionally high sources. New Zealand blackcurrants, grown in our UV-rich environment, have among the highest anthocyanin concentrations of any commercially grown berry in the world. Anthocyanins are among the most potent polyphenolic anti-inflammatories we know of, and emerging research on NZ blackcurrant extract specifically shows measurable reductions in inflammatory markers alongside improvements in cognitive function and recovery. CurraNZ — a concentrated NZ blackcurrant supplement — is one of the most clinically researched forms available if therapeutic levels are the goal.

🦠 Gut Health, Specifically

If the gut is a primary source of inflammatory signalling, supporting gut integrity directly is one of the highest-leverage things you can do. This means: adequate dietary fibre from diverse plant sources (aim for 30 different plant foods per week — more variety, not just more volume), fermented foods daily if tolerated (kefir, yoghurt, kimchi, sauerkraut), and minimising the gut disruptors that increase permeability: chronic NSAID use, alcohol, ultra-processed food, and chronic stress.

A high-quality probiotic with evidence-based strains (look for Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG or Bifidobacterium longum) has shown measurable effects on anxiety symptoms in several randomised controlled trials — via the gut-brain axis, not direct neurological action. The effect size is modest but real.

💊 Key Nutrients

Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) are among the most consistently anti-inflammatory nutrients we have. EPA in particular has direct anti-inflammatory effects in the brain and has shown anxiolytic effects in multiple trials. At least 2g of combined EPA/DHA daily from either oily fish or a high-quality supplement.

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including those regulating the stress response and GABA activity. Deficiency — extremely common in women, especially those under ongoing stress — directly increases inflammatory markers and anxiety. Magnesium glycinate or threonate are the most bioavailable forms. 300–400mg before bed.

Vitamin D is not just a bone health nutrient — it's an immune regulator and directly modulates inflammatory gene expression. Deficiency is widespread in New Zealand women, particularly in winter in the South Island and in anyone who works predominantly indoors. Get your levels checked. If below 75 nmol/L, supplement.

😴 Sleep Is Not Optional Here

A single night of poor sleep raises IL-6 and TNF-α measurably by the following morning. Chronic sleep deprivation keeps inflammatory markers persistently elevated — which keeps neuroinflammation elevated — which makes sleep harder. It's one of the cruellest cycles in women's health, and it's particularly common in perimenopause when night sweats, racing thoughts, and cortisol dysregulation all conspire against rest. Addressing sleep is not a lifestyle suggestion. If inflammation is a driver of your anxiety, sleep is a clinical intervention.

🏃‍♀️ Movement — The Right Kind

Moderate, consistent exercise is one of the most powerful anti-inflammatory interventions available without a prescription. It reduces CRP, IL-6, and TNF-α, increases BDNF (a neuroprotective growth factor), and has direct anxiolytic effects. The caveat is intensity: chronic high-intensity exercise without adequate recovery can itself become a source of systemic inflammation. For women with already elevated inflammatory burden, gentler, more consistent movement — walking, swimming, yoga, Pilates — often produces better outcomes than attempting to grind through fatigue with intense training.

What to Ask Your Doctor in New Zealand

If you think inflammation might be a factor in your anxiety, it's worth asking your GP for a basic inflammatory panel alongside your standard blood work. This typically includes CRP (C-reactive protein) and ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate). A full blood count, fasting glucose, and HbA1c are also useful, as insulin resistance is a major driver of systemic inflammation and is often underdiagnosed in women.

Thyroid function is worth checking too — hypothyroidism and Hashimoto's thyroiditis (an autoimmune thyroid condition that is significantly more common in women, especially in perimenopause) both produce anxiety symptoms and are associated with elevated inflammation. Many women in NZ have normal TSH but unaddressed thyroid autoimmunity that is contributing to both inflammation and mood disruption.

If you're perimenopausal and experiencing new or worsening anxiety, it's also worth discussing this in that context — not as a reason to dismiss the inflammation question, but because addressing declining oestrogen and progesterone is a complementary intervention that works through some of the same pathways.

A note

None of this is an argument against therapy, mindfulness, or medication. Those tools work, and they work better when the underlying physiological environment supports them. Think of reducing inflammation as setting the stage — it doesn't replace the other work, but it makes it more likely to land.

The Thing I Wish Someone Had Told Me

In clinic, I've worked with women who spent years managing anxiety as a purely psychological problem — therapy, medication, mindfulness — with partial results. When we addressed their inflammatory picture, something shifted that the other interventions hadn't reached.

That's not an argument against therapy or medication. It's an argument for looking at the whole body. The gut, the diet, the sleep, the immune burden — these are not separate from the mind. They are in constant conversation with it.

The women who do best are the ones who stop treating their anxiety as a character flaw or a brain defect, and start asking: what is my body trying to tell me?

That's information. And information is the beginning of choice.

Your anxiety is real. And it may have a body as well as a mind. Both deserve to be looked after.

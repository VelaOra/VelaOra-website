What You Need to Know

Perimenopause Starts Earlier Than You Think

In New Zealand, the average age of menopause is 51–52. But perimenopause — the hormonal shift leading up to it — can begin in your mid-to-late forties, or earlier. The erratic shifts in oestrogen and progesterone that cause every symptom you're about to read about begin years before your periods stop.

A national survey found that 89% of NZ women aged 40–60 have experienced new or worsening symptoms — yet women are routinely leaving GP appointments with antidepressants, eye drops, and stress management suggestions, their hormones unexamined.

A normal blood test does not rule out perimenopause. Your symptoms are data. Trust them.

— Sarah, VelaOra

Symptoms Nobody Connects

Hot flushes are expected. These are not:

3am wake-ups Anxiety from nowhere Brain fog Itchy ears Dry eyes Joint pain Heart palpitations Rage Changed body composition Tinnitus Low libido Fatigue

What To Do Right Now

Track your symptoms with dates before your GP appointment. Use the word "perimenopause" explicitly. Know that a normal FSH test doesn't close the case — oestrogen fluctuates daily in perimenopause, so one blood test is one data point. If your GP dismisses you, you are entitled to seek another opinion.

🥗 Nutrition for Perimenopause

Eat to support your hormones

What you eat has a direct impact on hormonal balance, inflammation, sleep quality, and bone health during perimenopause. These are the foundations:

Phytoestrogens Flaxseed, edamame, tempeh, and legumes contain plant oestrogens that may ease hormonal fluctuation. Add ground flaxseed to porridge or smoothies daily.
Calcium-Rich Foods Bone loss accelerates in perimenopause. Dairy, leafy greens, sardines, and fortified plant milks support bone density alongside vitamin D supplementation.
Omega-3 Fatty Acids Oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, and chia seeds reduce inflammation and support mood. Aim for fatty fish 2–3 times weekly.
Reduce Alcohol & Caffeine Both worsen sleep disruption and anxiety. Even one drink disrupts the sleep architecture that consolidates memory and mood.
Magnesium-Rich Foods NZ soil is low in magnesium. Dark chocolate, pumpkin seeds, spinach, and almonds help — but most women benefit from a quality magnesium glycinate supplement too.
Protein at Every Meal Supports muscle maintenance as body composition changes. Aim for 25–30g protein per meal — eggs, legumes, fish, quality dairy, or lean meat.

Menopause

Menopause Is Not an Ending

For many women, it is the beginning of the most powerful chapter of their lives. With the right information and support.

Understanding Menopause

What Nobody Told You About HRT

Menopause is defined as 12 consecutive months without a period. It's a hormonal milestone, not a diagnosis — yet millions of women reach it undertreated, underinformed, and unnecessarily suffering.

The old fear of HRT — driven by a 2002 study that has since been substantially reinterpreted — left a generation of women without treatment they were entitled to. The current evidence, led by specialists like Dr Louise Newson and reflected in the Australian Senate Inquiry into Menopause, tells a different story.

Modern HRT — The Facts

Body-identical oestrogen patches (transdermal oestrogen) are considered the gold standard — they bypass the liver and carry significantly different risk profiles to older oral HRT. Progesterone, testosterone, and topical oestrogen for vaginal health are all part of the modern menopause toolkit that NZ women deserve access to and information about.

Testosterone — The Conversation NZ Isn't Having

Testosterone therapy for women — low libido, energy, cognitive function — is prescribed routinely in the UK and increasingly in Australia. In NZ, many GPs will not prescribe it simply because they haven't been trained in it. This is not a safety issue. It is a knowledge gap. You are entitled to ask, and to seek a specialist if your GP won't discuss it.

Questions to ask your GP

Have you considered body-identical (transdermal) oestrogen? What is your view on testosterone for women? Can you refer me to a menopause specialist if this isn't your area? Is there a reason I shouldn't try HRT for three months and see how I feel?

🥗 Nutrition for Menopause

Nourishing your body through and beyond menopause

Post-menopause brings specific nutritional priorities. Bone density, cardiovascular health, and cognitive function all benefit from targeted nutritional support.

Bone Health Stack Calcium (1200mg/day from food), Vitamin D3 + K2 (supplement), and weight-bearing exercise. NZ fracture rates in postmenopausal women are among the highest in the developed world.
Heart Health Cardiovascular risk increases post-menopause. Mediterranean diet principles — olive oil, fish, legumes, vegetables — have the strongest evidence base.
Collagen Support Skin, joint, and connective tissue changes are real. Vitamin C (for collagen synthesis), collagen peptides, and adequate protein all support structural integrity.
Gut Health Hormonal changes shift gut microbiome composition. Fermented foods (yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut), prebiotic vegetables, and fibre support gut health and reduce inflammation.

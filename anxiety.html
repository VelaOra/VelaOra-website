Understanding Anxiety

Hormonal Anxiety Is Real — And Different

Anxiety that arrives in the perimenopausal years often has a different quality to the anxiety most women recognise from earlier life. It may arrive overnight, without a clear trigger, and feel biochemical rather than psychological — because it often is.

Oestrogen modulates serotonin, GABA, and the stress response system. As it fluctuates and declines, the nervous system becomes more reactive. This is not a mental health crisis. It is a hormonal shift — and it responds to hormonal treatment, lifestyle intervention, and targeted nutritional support.

The anxiety you feel at 3am that has no name and no cause is not weakness. It is a nervous system working with a different hormonal landscape than it had last year.

— VelaOra

Common presentations of hormonal anxiety

Racing thoughts at 3–4am · Heart palpitations · Sudden dread with no trigger · Irritability and rage that feels unlike you · Overwhelm at things that never bothered you before · Heightened startle response

Understanding Anxiety

Three Types. Different Approaches.

Many women need support across more than one type — and there is no shame in that. The goal is accurate identification so the right support can be offered.

Hormonal

Cyclical, Biochemical

Tends to be cyclical (worse around ovulation or before a period). Arrives alongside other perimenopause symptoms. Often responds meaningfully to HRT. Began in the 40s without prior anxiety history.

Situational

Context-Driven

Linked to specific stressors — separation, work, grief, uncertainty. Proportionate to what's happening, even if intense. Usually resolves as circumstances change. May benefit from targeted support during the crisis.

Clinical

Persistent, Independent

Persists independent of hormonal cycle or circumstances. Often has a longer history. May require psychological support (CBT, therapy) alongside any hormonal treatment. Worth getting a formal assessment.

What Genuinely Helps

Evidence-Based, Honestly Ranked

Address the underlying hormonal driver. For women whose anxiety began in perimenopause, HRT often produces the most substantial and rapid relief — because the root cause is hormonal, not psychological. This is the conversation many NZ GPs still aren't having.

Magnesium glycinate before bed has meaningful evidence for anxiety reduction, particularly for the 3am wake-up pattern. Not magnesium oxide — the glycinate form is the one that crosses the blood-brain barrier.

Address sleep disruption first. Sleep deprivation is one of the most powerful drivers of anxiety — and for perimenopausal women, much of the anxiety is significantly worsened by chronic, disrupted sleep. Treating the sleep often treats the anxiety.

Resistance training has robust evidence for anxiety regulation in midlife women. Stacy Sims' research is particularly compelling on this. Three sessions per week changes the neurochemistry meaningfully.

What to say to your GP

"I'd like to explore whether my anxiety has a hormonal component before going down the antidepressant route. Can we discuss HRT?" — This is a reasonable, evidence-based request. You are entitled to ask it.

🥗 Nutrition for Anxiety

Feeding your nervous system

The gut-brain axis is real — what you eat directly influences your anxiety levels through neurotransmitter production, inflammation regulation, and blood sugar stability.

Blood Sugar Stability Blood sugar crashes amplify anxiety significantly. Protein and healthy fat at every meal, reducing refined carbohydrates, and never skipping breakfast.
Magnesium-Rich Foods Dark chocolate, pumpkin seeds, spinach, and almonds. Most women in perimenopause are deficient — food alone often isn't enough, but it's the foundation.
Gut Health Foundation 90% of serotonin is produced in the gut. Fermented foods, prebiotic fibre, and reducing ultra-processed foods supports the gut-brain axis that regulates mood and anxiety.
Reduce Caffeine After Noon Caffeine has a 5–7 hour half-life and directly activates the stress response system. Particularly disruptive for hormonal anxiety. Try matcha or green tea for a gentler lift.
